About Cabbage Maggot

Cabbage maggot is a pest of cabbage, broccoli, radish, turnips and related (cruciferous) crops and weeds. Adult flies lay their eggs at the base of plants and larvae tunnel in stems and roots, damaging marketable portions, interrupting the flow of water and nutrients, and creating wounds for pathogens and secondary organisms. Light infestations can kill small seedlings and transplants, delay plant development, or render root crops like radish unsalable; high populations can kill older plants or reduce yield. There are four generations per year in New York.

How to Use Cabbage Maggot Forecasts

Adult flights (emergence) can be predicted based on degree-day (base 40°F) accumulations. Highest risk of infestation is typically during peak emergence (50%) from the first and second adult flights, though later generations can also be damaging. Peak flights are indicated by the "50%" red dotted lines on the degree-day accumulation chart. Seedlings, transplants or root crops present around the time of peak flights should be protected by an insecticide treatment or with row covers. Insecticides are applied just prior to planting; row covers must be in place well before adults begin to emerge. See the Cornell Integrated Crop and Pest Management Guidelines for Commercial Vegetable Production for more information.

Risk of infestation in outdoor production is negligible after October 15, so row covers can be removed at that time.
